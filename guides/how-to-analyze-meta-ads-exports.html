How to analyze Meta Ads exports

Meta exports are useful, but they often mix prospecting and retargeting, lag conversions, and hide the real budget story behind noisy naming conventions.

Meta AdsProspectingRetargetingSignal quality

Why this matters

  • Meta exports often look simple at first and messy once you compare campaigns side by side.
  • Prospecting and retargeting should not be judged with the same expectations.
  • A single ROAS number rarely tells the whole budget story.

Common mistakes

  • Using spend alone as the decision rule.
  • Mixing retargeting with prospecting when you compare performance.
  • Treating every low ROAS campaign as wasted spend without checking volume.

What strong vs weak signal looks like

  • Strong: spend, conversions, and revenue all point in the same direction.
  • Weak: low spend, delayed conversions, or a noisy split between campaign types.
  • Thin: enough data to observe, but not enough to move aggressively.

How Gnomeo approaches it

  • Normalizes messy campaign names into a consistent review layer.
  • Compares campaigns against the account, not just against each other.
  • Stays conservative when the signal is too thin to trust safely.

Reduce £2,400.00 from Meta Prospecting Broad UK → increase Google Brand Search by £2,400.00.

Why conservative analysis matters

Meta exports can make a campaign look better or worse depending on attribution lag, audience overlap, and how much conversion signal actually landed in the report. Conservative analysis protects you from overcorrecting on a noisy week.
